The Human Person as an Embodied Spirit

Understanding the Union of Body and Soul

Core Concepts

  • Embodied Human Person: A being where mind and body are one, living with limitations (immanence) but able to go beyond them (transcendence).
  • Embodiment: The state of being alive in a physical body, which is essential to all human experience.
  • "I am my body" (Marcel): The body is not something we possess, but an inseparable part of our identity.
  • Phenomenological View (Heidegger): The body is a lived experience, not just a biological object.

Views on the Soul

  • Aristotle: The soul is the "form" of the body, the principle of life that enables nutrition, sensation, movement, and thought.
  • St. Thomas Aquinas: The soul is an incorporeal, subsistent (exists in itself), and incorruptible (cannot be destroyed) principle that animates the body.

Limitations & Possibilities for Transcendence

Going Beyond Our Limits

Immanence vs. Transcendence

Immanence: Existing within the bounds of the world and human experience.

Transcendence: The capacity to go beyond current limitations toward greater possibilities.

Philosophical Views on Immanence

  • Plato: A particular object (a specific dog) is an instance of a universal form (the idea of "dog").
  • Kant: What is within the limits of human cognition and understanding.
  • Husserl: Anything present in one's immediate consciousness or awareness.

St. Augustine & St. Thomas Aquinas

On Will and Love

Eternal Law (St. Augustine)

God's divine law that commands all beings to do good and avoid evil, forming the ultimate basis of moral obligation.

Moral Freedom

Humans have the physical freedom to act, but also the moral responsibility to use that freedom to choose what is right and good.

Chastity

A virtue of purity and holiness that is required of all people, regardless of their state in life (single or married), as a form of love and respect.

The Human Person and the Environment

Philosophical Approaches to Ecology

Human Approaches to the Environment

  • Instrumental: Protecting nature only for human benefit.
  • Axiological: Recognizing nature's intrinsic, independent value.
  • Anthropological: Viewing nature as an object for human manipulation.
  • Utilitarianism: Actions are right if they produce the greatest good for the greatest number.
  • Natural Law Ethics: Nature is inherently good as God's creation, and we must care for it.
  • Virtue Ethics: Developing virtues like care and respect for the environment.

Ethical Frameworks

  • Stewardship (P. Dobel): Humans are caretakers, not masters, of the Earth.
  • Land Ethics (A. Leopold): Humans are members, not conquerors, of the land-community.
  • Biocentric Egalitarianism (P. Taylor): A life-centered view where all living things have equal moral worth.

The Biocentric Outlook (Four Components)

  1. Humans are equal members of Earth's community of life.
  2. Earth's ecosystems are interconnected and interdependent.
  3. Each organism pursues its own good in its own way.
  4. Human superiority is a groundless claim.

A Quick Glossary

Anthropocentrism: Human-centered worldview.
Axiological Approach: Valuing nature for its own sake.
Biocentric Egalitarianism: Belief that all living things have equal moral worth.
Biocentric Outlook: A life-centered perspective on the world.
Chastity: A virtue of purity and holiness.
Contact of Power: Interaction between two physical bodies.
Contact of Quality: Interaction between material and immaterial (e.g., soul and body).
Ecological Conscience: Awareness and responsibility for nature's health.
Ecological Land Ethics: Viewing humans as members of the land-community.
Embodied Human Person: A being where mind and body are one.
Embodiment: The state of living in a physical body.
Environmental Crisis: Critical damage to the environment threatening life.
Eternal Law: God's law to do good and avoid evil.
Humility in Ecology: Recognizing humanity's equality with other life forms.
Immanence: Existing within the limits of the world or consciousness.
Incorruptible Soul: A soul that cannot be destroyed.
Instrumental Worth: The value of nature based on its usefulness to humans.
Intrinsic Value of Nature: Nature is valuable in itself.
Moral Freedom: The freedom to act, with the responsibility to choose what is right.
Natural Law Ethics: Belief that nature is inherently good as God's creation.
Phenomenological View: Seeing the body as a lived experience, not just an object.
Principle of Intrinsic Value: Each member of the community of life has value in itself.
Principle of Moral Consideration: All wild living things deserve concern.
Soul: The principle of life that animates the body.
Subsistent Soul: A soul that can exist independently of the body.
Transcendence: The capacity to go beyond one's limitations.
Utilitarianism: Actions are right if they produce the greatest happiness for the greatest number.
Virtue Ethics Perspective: Emphasizes developing virtues like care and respect for the environment.
Will to Power: The drive to overcome oneself and life's challenges.
