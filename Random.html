The Nature of Philosophy

From the Greek Philos (Love) and Sophia (Wisdom), philosophy is formally defined as the science that by natural light of reason studies the first causes or highest principles of all things.

A Science

It is a systematic investigation following specific procedures to create an organized body of knowledge.

Natural Light of Reason

It uses unaided human reason alone, not instruments or supernatural revelation.

Study of All Things

It is holistic, questioning everything, unlike partial sciences that focus on one object of study.

First Cause or Highest Principle

The Four Pillars of Thought

These are the fundamental laws of logic that provide the foundation for all rational thought.

Identity

Whatever is, is; whatever is not, is not. Everything is its own being.

Non-Contradiction

It is impossible for a thing to be and not be at the same time and in the same respect.

Excluded Middle

A thing either is or is not; there is no middle ground possible between being and not-being.

Sufficient Reason

Nothing exists without a sufficient reason for its being and existence.

A Journey Through Time

Explore the evolution of Western philosophy, from ancient myths to modern inquiries into the human condition.

Pre-Philosophical Period

Before formal philosophy, ancient cultures (Babylonians, Chinese, Hindus, Egyptians) explained the world through myths, legends, and supernatural powers.

Pre-Socratic Period

The dawn of rational inquiry. Thales (Water), Anaximander (Apeiron), Anaximenes (Air), and Pythagoras (Numbers) sought the single, underlying substance of the universe.

Socrates, Plato & Aristotle

Socrates used his questioning method. Plato championed reason and the world of Forms, arguing for philosopher kings in *The Republic*. Aristotle, philosophizing from wonder, focused on empirical observation.

Medieval Period

The age of Scholasticism. St. Anselm (ontological argument), St. Augustine (argument by analogy), and St. Thomas Aquinas (*Summa Theologica*) used reason to serve faith.

Modern Period

The era of epistemology. Rationalists (Descartes, Spinoza, Leibniz) clashed with Empiricists (Locke), a debate ultimately synthesized by Kant.

Contemporary Period

Analytic (Russell, Moore, Wittgenstein) focused on logic and language. Continental (Existentialism, Phenomenology) focused on subjective experience and history.

The Great Debate: How We Know

The clash between reason and experience defined modern philosophy until a titan of thought offered a synthesis.

Rationalism
Knowledge is innate (a priori)
Empiricism
Knowledge is experienced (a posteriori)

Kant's Synthesis

Reason structures experience.

A New Path Forward

Rationalists like Descartes argued we are born with knowledge. Empiricists like Locke said our minds are a blank slate. Immanuel Kant brilliantly merged them, stating that our minds have innate structures (reason) that organize the raw data from our senses (experience).

A Filipino Perspective

Exploring unique cultural concepts that shape the Filipino worldview.

Loob

A holistic sense of one's inner self (kagandahang-loob, kabutihang-loob). It emphasizes shared humanity and duties over rights.

Bahala Na

"Leave it to God (Bathala)." Not passive resignation, but a courageous trust in Divine Providence after one has done their best.

Filipino Time & Optimism

Belief in Gulong ng Palad (Wheel of Fortune) fosters an unmitigated optimism. "Filipino time" is not laziness, but a different cultural rhythm.

Partial Point of View

Looking at a situation using a single perspective or a limited set of factors. An incomplete picture.

Holistic Perspective

Seeing the "big picture" by understanding the connections between all parts of a whole. A more complete understanding.
