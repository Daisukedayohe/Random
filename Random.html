The Four Pillars of Thought

These are the fundamental laws of logic that provide the foundation for all rational thought.

Identity

Whatever is, is. A thing is itself.

Non-Contradiction

Something cannot be true and false at the same time.

Excluded Middle

A statement is either true or false, with no middle ground.

Sufficient Reason

Everything must have a reason or a cause for its existence.

A Journey Through Time

Explore the evolution of Western philosophy, from the ancient questions about the cosmos to modern inquiries into the human condition.

Pre-Socratic Period

The dawn of philosophy. Thinkers like Thales, Anaximander, and Pythagoras sought the single, underlying substance of the universe (the *arche*).

Socratic Period

A shift from cosmology to humanity. Socrates introduced his method of questioning, and his student Plato championed reason as the path to truth.

Aristotle

Plato's student, who took a different path. Aristotle was an empiricist, believing that knowledge is derived from our sensory experiences of the world.

Medieval Period

The age of Scholasticism. Philosophers like St. Augustine and St. Thomas Aquinas worked to synthesize philosophy with Christian theology.

Modern Period

The era of epistemology. The central conflict was between Rationalism (knowledge from reason) and Empiricism (knowledge from experience).

Contemporary Period

Philosophy splits into two major traditions: the Analytic (focused on logic and language) and the Continental (focused on subjective experience).

The Great Debate: How We Know

The clash between reason and experience defined modern philosophy until a titan of thought offered a synthesis.

Rationalism
Knowledge is innate (a priori)
Empiricism
Knowledge is experienced (a posteriori)

Kant's Synthesis

Reason structures experience.

A New Path Forward

Rationalists like Plato argued we are born with knowledge. Empiricists like Locke said our minds are a blank slate. Immanuel Kant brilliantly merged them, stating that our minds have innate structures (reason) that organize the raw data from our senses (experience).

The Anatomy of Philosophy

Philosophy is a vast field with several major branches, each asking a different fundamental question about our world and ourselves.

A Filipino Perspective

Exploring unique cultural concepts that shape the Filipino worldview.

Loob

The inner self; a holistic concept of one's core being and innate goodness.

Bahala Na!

A courageous faith. Trusting in a higher power after one has done their absolute best.

Gulong ng Palad

The "Wheel of Fortune." The belief that life is cyclical, with inevitable ups and downs.

Partial Perspective

Viewing reality based on a single factor or context. Limited and subjective.

Holistic Perspective

Seeing the "big picture" by considering all aspects and their interconnections. Broader and more synthesized.
