Part 1: The 5 Components of Fitness

Understanding Your Physical Health

Body Composition (BMI)

Refers to the percentage of fat, bone, and muscle in your body. Body Mass Index (BMI) is a common screening tool, calculated as `Weight (kg) / Height (m)²`.

  • Underweight: < 18.5
  • Normal weight: 18.5 – 24.9
  • Overweight: 25.0 – 29.9
  • Obese: ≥ 30.0

Cardiovascular Endurance

The ability of your heart and lungs to supply oxygen to your body during sustained physical activity.

Muscular Strength

The maximum amount of force a muscle can produce in a single effort (e.g., lifting a heavy weight).

Muscular Endurance

The ability of the muscles to perform continuously without fatiguing over a period of time.

Flexibility

The ability of your joints to move through a full range of motion, which helps prevent injuries.

Part 2: Designing Your Workout

The FITT Principle & Core Training Concepts

The FITT Principle

Frequency

How often you exercise (e.g., 3-5 times a week).

Intensity

How hard you exercise (e.g., light, moderate, vigorous).

Time

How long you exercise (e.g., 30 minutes per session).

Type

The specific kind of exercise you do (e.g., running, swimming, lifting).

Key Principles of Exercise

Overload: To improve, you must consistently increase the stress on your body over time.

Progression: Start slowly and increase the amount and intensity of exercise gradually and safely.

Specificity: Your training should be relevant and appropriate to your specific goals.

Variety: Varying your workout prevents boredom, plateaus, and overuse injuries.

Rest & Recovery: Your body needs time to adapt and repair between workouts to get stronger.

Reversibility: Fitness gains are lost with inactivity. If you don't use it, you lose it!

Individuality: Everyone responds to exercise differently based on genetics, age, and experience.

Part 3: Monitoring Your Effort

Physiological Indicators of Exertion

Heart Rate (Pulse)

The number of times your heart beats per minute. A higher heart rate indicates greater effort.

Pacing

The rate or speed at which you perform an activity. Proper pacing helps you manage your energy and complete your workout effectively.

Rate of Perceived Exertion (RPE)

A subjective measure of how hard you feel you're working, based on heart rate, breathing, and fatigue.

  • 6 No exertion at all
  • 7.5 Extremely light
  • 9 Very light (e.g., gentle walk)
  • 11 Light
  • 13 Slightly hard, but manageable
  • 15 Hard
  • 17 Very hard
  • 20 Maximal exertion / Complete exhaustion

Part 4: Careers in Health & Fitness

Turning Passion into a Profession

Physical Education Teacher

Educate students in physical fitness and health.

Sports Administration

Manage the business side of sports teams or leagues.

Personal Trainer

Provide one-on-one or group fitness instruction.

Coach

Lead and train athletic teams to compete.

Sports Research

Study and develop new knowledge in sports science.

Sports Psychologist

Work with athletes on the mental aspects of performance.

Dietitian

Provide expert nutrition consulting and create wellness programs.
