Overview — Why the Philippines is vulnerable

ITCZ • Tropical

The Philippines sits near the Inter-tropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ), where trade winds meet and create strong swirling atmospheric movement. Combined with archipelagic coastlines and mountainous terrain, this causes frequent hydrometeorological and geological hazards.

Hydrometeorological Hazards

Weather-, ocean- and atmosphere-driven hazards. Definitions, examples, monitoring, and what to do before/during/after.

Typhoon (Bagyo)

Definition: Intense circulating winds over tropical waters and land. Slides describe "intense" as winds > 118 km/h.

Life stages (PAGASA, 2021)

  • Formative: Disorganized clouds, no eye/spiral bands.
  • Immature: Clouds curve toward a forming center; pressure drops.
  • Mature: Circulation expands; max wind reached; pressure stabilizes.
  • Decaying: System dissipates; pressure rises.

PSWS (Public Storm Warning Signal): Used to warn communities — exact numeric thresholds are available from PAGASA.

Thunderstorm

Electrical storm with heavy rain, thunder and lightning. May not have strong winds like typhoons.

Lightning vs Thunder

Lightning is electrical discharge; thunder is the sound from rapid air expansion.

Flood & Flash Flood

Flood: Excessive water on normally dry land; common in low-lying areas and poor drainage systems.

Flash Flood: Rapid water flow on saturated or non-absorptive ground — often on slopes and waterways causing fast downstream peaks.

Storm Surge

An abnormal rise in coastal waters caused by strong typhoon winds or sea movement (seaquake). Can rapidly inundate coastal zones.

Tornado / Whirlwind

Rapidly rotating column of air contacting land. Slides report an average of 12–24 tornadoes/year in the Philippines — common in flat terrains such as Mindanao and Central Luzon.

El Niño & La Niña

El Niño: Large-scale warming of the central/east-central Equatorial Pacific — can increase drought risk.

La Niña: Cooling of the same region; SST can be ~3–5 °C lower and often leads to heavier rainfall.

Monitoring tools: Doppler Radar (rainfall, wind speed/direction), Automated Rain Gauge (often installed at telecom cell sites for early detection of heavy rainfall and flood/flash-flood risk).

Preparedness & Response (Hydromet)

Before:

  • Store enough food, drinking water, and medicine.
  • Have working flashlight, spare batteries, battery radio, candles, and matches ready.
  • Make evacuation plans — know higher/safe ground and routes.
  • Move valuables and appliances to higher ground if flood-prone.
  • Secure roof, doors and windows if typhoon is forecast.

During:

  • If not advised to evacuate: stay indoors and keep calm.
  • Listen to PAGASA and NDRRMC advisories; use battery radio if power is out.
  • If water rises: unplug appliances, turn off main circuit breaker.
  • Wear protective clothing when evacuating and follow authorities' guidance.

After:

  • Wait for official announcements before returning home.
  • Report fallen power lines or blocked roads to authorities.
  • Check house for damages — do not re-enter if heavily damaged.

Geological Hazards

Landslide (Landslip)

Downward movement of soil/rock on slopes. Rain is a natural agent that saturates soil and triggers landslides.

Contributors / Causes (Slides)

  1. Springs, water seepage, or water-saturated ground.
  2. Soil loosening or loss of cohesion near foundations.
  3. New cracks, unusual bulges on cemented ground or walls.
  4. Broken underground utility lines due to vibrations.
  5. Buildings, poles, walls, fences that start leaning.
  6. Sudden rapid flow of creek waters carrying silted material.

Sinkhole

Depressions or holes formed by collapse of the surface layer — common where subsurface rock is dissolved or weakened by water.

Formation

Sinkholes form when groundwater dissolves subsurface layers or when soft beds cannot support soil, especially after repeated flooding.

Signs of Impending Failure

Landslide signs:

  • New springs or water seepage.
  • Soil loosening or ground moving away from foundations.
  • New cracks or bulges on ground/walls.
  • Leaning buildings, poles, fences.
  • Sudden rapid flow of creeks with more silt.

Sinkhole signs:

  • Groundwater becomes cloudy, acidic or unusually silted.
  • Posts/signs suddenly leaning or slumping.
  • New ponds or puddles that persist or grow.

Scientific Tools

  • Landslides: Abney level, laser range finder — for slope and movement measurement.
  • Sinkholes: Ground-Penetrating Radar (GPR) to detect subsurface voids.

Precautionary & Mitigation Measures

If area is prone to landslide:

  • Plant more trees, grasses and vegetation to prevent erosion.
  • Build diversion channels for mudflow/debris.
  • Do not build houses near steep slopes, mountain edges, or drainage paths.

During a landslide:

  • Be attentive to unusual sounds (cracking, moving debris).
  • Stay away from the path of debris; get to elevated, sturdy ground.
  • If escape not possible: curl into a ball, protect head.

After a landslide:

  • Avoid slide area due to risk of additional slides.
  • Watch for flooding along the debris path.
  • Check for injured/trapped people and report to rescuers.
